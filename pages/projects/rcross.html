Background

Background

The Red Cross is one of the nation's first disaster relief organizations. The Red Cross provides emergency assistance and services during disasters. In addition, they provide medical support during other events. Like many other emergency services organizations, the command systems to record events and communicate with command are antiquated, leaving room for improvement.

Challenge

During certain events, the American Red Cross command staff experience difficulties in tracking the movements of on-the-ground responders. The current method of tracking members creates communication delays between command and response teams, reducing cohesion and hindering effectiveness during time-sensitive operations. Thus, the team set out to develop a technical solution that could be utilized by emergency responders during special events to better document incidents and communicate with command.

Objectives

  • Establish the designs for an updated communication system.
  • Develop a functional prototype and conduct UX research.

UX Research

My Role

I developed the wireframes for the user view of the application utilizing Figma. I was also involved in various parts of the user research portion of development, including contextual research, card sorting, tree testing, and usability testing.

Contextual Research

The team began with contextual research to develop baseline knowledge on the types of injuries occurring at large-scale events, EMS best practices at large events, and the features present in incident command systems (ICS). Mass gatherings can include concerts, sporting events, religious celebrations, street fairs, parades, and political rallies. Each event is unique, and estimation of necessary resources can be derived from previous events of a similar nature. At large-scale events, typical injuries include crush injuries, traumatic brain injuries, burns, lacerations, heat exhaustion/dehydration, and substance abuse. To reduce the risk of injury, it’s crucial for emergency responders to have a well-developed plan prior to providing medical care at events. The utmost priority of emergency responders during events is scene safety and personnel safety. Lastly, it should be discussed with the organizer whether post-event medical care will be provided.

Unstructured Interviews

Unstructured Interviews were conducted with two Red Cross members. The interviews provided insight into the current procedure for special event communication. Depending on the event, volunteers must coordinate with nearby hospitals, deploy in high-risk locations, and attend a Red Cross briefing the day before the event. During an event, volunteers typically engage in hourly communication with command regarding team status, location, and observations. Following the conclusion of an event, Go Teams must provide post-event updates on crowd movements or activities.

Card Sorting

Participants found that the wording used for “report and emergency” and “document an event” was unclear. One participant mentioned that “document an event” was far too broad and needed to be broken down into additional, more descriptive categories. Some suggestions for improved clarification included having “documentation” as an overarching category, and within that category, include documentation for admin (prep work, liaison work) and documentation for users (list emergency situation progress, record notes). Updates included a revamped navigation bar, display of status information as a timeline as opposed to icons, display of GPS location of assistance on the track status page, splitting reports into active and resolved emergencies, including announcements, check-in, and crowd condition reporting functionality.

Concept Testing

Concept testing was conducted with the low-fidelity prototype; participants included members of RPI Ambulance and personnel at the local firehouse. The feedback highlighted pain points in form fields and documentation categories. The vast majority of documentation for events fell into a fairly predictable set of categories. Thus, the field for documenting an event was changed from a write-in field to a radio button format. This would allow for greater efficiency when logging events and standardization of data entry.

Usability Testing

Usability testing was conducted with a wide variety of potential users, including trained Red Cross volunteers, first responders, and several individuals who were completely new to the system. Testing was conducted with the finalized high-fidelity prototype. The vast majority of users were able to complete the assigned tasks, demonstrating a minimal learning curve for the application. Other minor changes included adjusting the expanded task view to a minus icon instead of an "x" to demonstrate minimizing and expanding tasks as opposed to opening and closing them. Additionally, the input method for reporting resolved events was changed from radio buttons to a dropdown to accommodate a large number of options.

Designs

Low Fidelity

Initial designs began as low-fidelity wireframes. These designs were developed following the information gained from secondary research and unstructured interviews. The designs initially featured a page to track the status of emergencies, reporting emergencies, and documenting an event. A low to medium fidelity update is pictured below.

Medium Fidelity

Following concept testing and card-sorting, designs were updated to medium-fidelity. The navigation bar was redesigned and more specified information was included. Additional functionality was added to the application including a section for announcements, check-in form, reporting crowd conditions functionality, and the ability to view and update submitted documents. The final set of tests, tree testing, and usability testing all led to the final design wireframes. The following image shows the updates for the track status page from medium fidelity to high fidelity.

High Fidelity

The navigation bar was finalized and track status was changed. Rather than display the timeline on the updates page, a general icon with the color and status were displayed. To view additional information, events could be expanded. To streamline reporting an emergency, a series of buttons were utilized. Users can select an emergency type to view a form with prefilled information to simplify the submission process. Document history was updated to an accordion format filtered by the specified event that was occurring. Documentation for the current day was listed at the top with filtering capabilities to see information from the last seven days.The expanded details for information regarding submitted documents mirror the design of the active updates view for design consistency. To match the user's mental models, the icon for the current location of a team was chosen as a flag to mirror teams placing flags into the ground to showcase their current position to ensure help could find them. Lessons Learned

  • Beware of developing UX research tasks that lead users to the end goal.
